In two days — 1 April 2026 — [KSeF becomes mandatory](/en/blog/ksef-od-1-kwietnia-2026) for all active VAT taxpayers in Poland. If you have clients abroad, the obligation applies to you just the same. An invoice for a buyer in Berlin, Amsterdam, or New York must go through KSeF — even if that buyer has no Polish NIP (tax identification number) and will never log in to the system.

A foreign buyer has no access to KSeF. You need to send them a readable copy of the invoice separately — by email, by post, however you did it before. It's extra work, but there's no way around it. This article walks you through the correct way to fill in a foreign buyer's details, choose the right VAT rate, and avoid having your invoice rejected.

If your invoicing software handles foreign counterparties — most of these fields will be filled in automatically. But if something goes wrong, or you want to understand why a particular invoice was rejected — you'll find the specifics here.

---

## EU buyer with a VAT-EU number

This is the most common scenario for freelancers and small businesses: you're invoicing a company in the European Union that holds an active EU VAT number.

In the buyer section (Podmiot2 in the FA(3) schema) you must use the pair of fields `KodUE` + `NrVatUE` — **not NIP**. The NIP field is reserved exclusively for Polish tax identification numbers. A foreign VAT number entered in the NIP field will cause KSeF to reject the invoice.

### Which fields to fill in

- **`KodUE`** — two-letter EU country prefix (e.g. `DE` for Germany, `NL` for the Netherlands, `FR` for France)
- **`NrVatUE`** — the buyer's VAT number **without the country prefix** (the number only — no leading "DE")
- **`Nazwa`** — the buyer's full company name

For the buyer's address, provide `KodKraju` (same country code as `KodUE`), `AdresL1` (street and number), and `AdresL2` (postcode and city).

<Tip>
  Before you issue the invoice, verify the buyer's EU VAT number in VIES — the European Commission's
  official database. An inactive number means trouble when settling intra-Community supplies of
  goods (WDT) or services.
</Tip>

Example — invoice for a company in Germany:

<XmlExample copyable>
  ```xml
  <Podmiot2>
    <DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
      <KodUE>DE</KodUE>
      <NrVatUE>123456789</NrVatUE>
      <Nazwa>Berlin Design GmbH</Nazwa>
    </DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
    <Adres>
      <KodKraju>DE</KodKraju>
      <AdresL1>Musterstraße 1</AdresL1>
      <AdresL2>10115 Berlin</AdresL2>
    </Adres>
    <JST>2</JST>
    <GV>2</GV>
  </Podmiot2>
  ```
</XmlExample>

`JST` and `GV` are mandatory fields in every Podmiot2 block in FA(3). For foreign counterparties, always set the value to `2` ("not applicable"). Omitting these fields will trigger a validation error.

## EU buyer without a VAT number

Not every EU counterparty has a VAT-EU number. A private individual, a flat-rate freelancer, a company not registered for VAT — in these cases you simply don't have a number to enter.

Instead of `KodUE` + `NrVatUE`, use the field `BrakID` with the value `1`. This means: "the buyer has no tax identification number." The `Nazwa` field remains mandatory.

<XmlExample copyable>
  ```xml
  <Podmiot2>
    <DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
      <BrakID>1</BrakID>
      <Nazwa>Anna Müller</Nazwa>
    </DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
    <Adres>
      <KodKraju>DE</KodKraju>
      <AdresL1>Hauptstraße 5</AdresL1>
      <AdresL2>80331 München</AdresL2>
    </Adres>
    <JST>2</JST>
    <GV>2</GV>
  </Podmiot2>
  ```
</XmlExample>

<Warning>
  If your EU buyer doesn't have a VAT-EU number, you cannot apply the `0 WDT` rate on goods or the
  `np II` rate on intra-Community services. The VAT treatment may be different — consult your
  accountant about the specific case.
</Warning>

## Non-EU buyer

A client in the US, the UK (post-Brexit), Switzerland, Canada — this is a separate category. You don't use `KodUE` or `NrVatUE`, because those fields apply exclusively to EU buyers.

For a non-EU buyer you have two identification options.

### Buyer has a foreign tax ID

Use the pair `KodKraju` + `NrID` inside the `DaneIdentyfikacyjne` block. These are two separate, sibling XML elements — not nested inside each other.

- **`KodKraju`** — two-letter ISO country code (e.g. `US`, `GB`, `CH`, `NO`)
- **`NrID`** — the foreign tax identification number (e.g. EIN in the US, UTR in the UK, UID in Switzerland)

Example — invoice for a company in the US:

<XmlExample copyable>
  ```xml
  <Podmiot2>
    <DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
      <KodKraju>US</KodKraju>
      <NrID>12-3456789</NrID>
      <Nazwa>US Design Inc.</Nazwa>
    </DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
    <Adres>
      <KodKraju>US</KodKraju>
      <AdresL1>123 Main Street</AdresL1>
      <AdresL2>New York, NY 10001</AdresL2>
    </Adres>
    <JST>2</JST>
    <GV>2</GV>
  </Podmiot2>
  ```
</XmlExample>

Note that `KodKraju` appears twice — once in `DaneIdentyfikacyjne` (tax identification context) and once in `Adres` (registered address). These are two separate fields in different sections, and both are required.

### Buyer has no tax ID

Same approach as for an EU buyer without a number — use `BrakID` with the value `1`:

<XmlExample copyable>
  ```xml
  <DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
    <BrakID>1</BrakID>
    <Nazwa>John Smith Freelance</Nazwa>
  </DaneIdentyfikacyjne>
  ```
</XmlExample>

<Info>
  The United Kingdom is no longer an EU member state. A UK-based counterparty is a non-EU buyer —
  use `KodKraju` = `GB` and `NrID`, not `KodUE` + `NrVatUE`.
</Info>

## VAT rates on foreign invoices

This is where mistakes happen most easily. The VAT rate on a foreign invoice depends on **what** you're selling (goods or services) and **to whom** (EU buyer or non-EU buyer).

| Transaction type              | Rate (P_12) | Net amount field | When to use                                                                                           |
| :---------------------------- | :---------- | :--------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| WDT — goods to the EU         | `0 WDT`     | `P_13_6_2`       | Sale of goods to a buyer with a VAT-EU number                                                         |
| Export of goods               | `0 EX`      | `P_13_6_3`       | Sale of goods to a non-EU buyer                                                                       |
| Services to EU businesses     | `np II`     | `P_13_9`         | Services under Art. 100 ust. 1 pkt 4 of the VAT Act — the buyer accounts for VAT in their own country |
| Services to non-EU businesses | `np I`      | `P_13_8`         | Services outside Polish territory (Art. 28b) — the buyer accounts for VAT                             |

### What does this look like in practice?

Building a website for a German client who has a VAT-EU number? → It's a service, the buyer accounts for VAT locally, rate: **`np II`**.

Building a website for a US client? → It's a service outside the EU, rate: **`np I`**.

Shipping a physical product to a client in the Netherlands (with a VAT-EU number)? → That's an intra-Community supply of goods (WDT), rate: **`0 WDT`**.

Shipping a physical product to a client in the UK? → That's an export of goods, rate: **`0 EX`**.

<Warning>
  The rate `oo` (domestic reverse charge) **never** applies to foreign counterparties. For EU and
  non-EU buyers, use `np I` or `np II`. Using `oo` for a foreign buyer is one of the most common
  mistakes — and a reason for rejection.
</Warning>

Rate values in FA(3) are **case-sensitive and space-sensitive**. Correct: `np I`, `np II`, `0 WDT`, `0 EX`. Wrong: `NP I`, `npI`, `0WDT`.

## Annotations — what to change for a foreign invoice

The Adnotacje (annotations) block in FA(3) contains mandatory declaration fields. Two of them are directly relevant to foreign invoices — and easy to confuse with each other:

| Field   | Value | Meaning                                                                                                                |
| :------ | :---- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `P_18`  | `1`   | Reverse charge — the buyer accounts for VAT (services at the `np I` or `np II` rate)                                   |
| `P_18`  | `2`   | No reverse charge (e.g. WDT at `0 WDT`, exports at `0 EX`)                                                             |
| `P_18A` | `1`   | Split payment mechanism (MPP) — amount exceeds 15,000 PLN and the goods/services are listed in Annex 15 of the VAT Act |
| `P_18A` | `2`   | No MPP — the default for foreign transactions                                                                          |

**`P_18` is reverse charge. `P_18A` is split payment (MPP).** These are two entirely different concepts — don't confuse them. MPP applies to domestic payments to a VAT account and has no bearing on foreign transactions.

For most foreign invoices for services: **`P_18` = `1`**, **`P_18A` = `2`**.

For WDT and export of goods: **`P_18` = `2`**, **`P_18A` = `2`**.

<Info>
  All eight fields in the Adnotacje block are mandatory — even when their value is "not applicable"
  (value `2` or the relevant negative marker). Omitting any of them will cause a validation error.
  We've covered the full Adnotacje structure in the article on [most common FA(3) validation
  errors](/en/blog/najczestsze-bledy-walidacji-fa3).
</Info>

## Foreign-currency invoices and foreign buyers

You can issue a foreign invoice in any currency — EUR, USD, GBP, CHF. The `KodWaluty` field in FA(3) accepts standard ISO 4217 currency codes. Net and gross amounts are stated in the chosen currency.

There is one exception: **the VAT amount must be expressed in PLN** — converted at the NBP (National Bank of Poland) mid-rate from the last business day before the tax point. For rates `np I`, `np II`, `0 WDT`, and `0 EX` the VAT amount is zero — so no conversion is needed. That covers the vast majority of foreign invoices.

## Common pitfalls

These are the mistakes that come up again and again on foreign-buyer invoices:

- **Foreign VAT number in the NIP field** — NIP is exclusively a Polish tax ID. For an EU buyer, use `KodUE` + `NrVatUE`; for a non-EU buyer, use `KodKraju` + `NrID`. Putting a foreign number in the NIP field → invoice rejected.

- **Country prefix in the VAT number** — the `NrVatUE` field takes the number only, **without** the `DE`, `NL`, `FR` prefix. The prefix goes separately in the `KodUE` field. Entering `DE123456789` in `NrVatUE` is an error.

- **Missing `KodKraju` in the address** — the `KodKraju` field inside the `Adres` block is required. Without it, the invoice won't pass validation.

- **Forgetting to send a copy to the buyer** — KSeF will not deliver the invoice to a foreign counterparty. You must send a readable copy yourself (a PDF with a QR verification code) by email or post. Without it, the buyer doesn't know the invoice exists.

- **Confusing WDT with export of services** — WDT applies only to goods shipped within the EU (rate `0 WDT`). Services for an EU counterparty are a different category (rate `np II`). Different rates, different net amount fields, different `P_18` value.

- **Using the `oo` rate for a foreign buyer** — `oo` is domestic reverse charge and applies exclusively to transactions within Poland. For foreign counterparties, the correct rate is `np I` or `np II`.

- **Treating the UK as an EU country** — since Brexit, the UK is not in the European Union. A UK counterparty requires `KodKraju` + `NrID`, not `KodUE` + `NrVatUE`.

## TL;DR

- An invoice for a foreign counterparty **must** go through KSeF — same as a domestic one.
- A foreign buyer has no access to KSeF — send them a readable copy separately (PDF with QR).
- EU buyer with a VAT-EU number → `KodUE` + `NrVatUE` (not NIP).
- EU buyer without a VAT number → `BrakID` = `1`.
- Non-EU buyer with a tax ID → `KodKraju` + `NrID`.
- Non-EU buyer without a tax ID → `BrakID` = `1`.
- Services to EU businesses → rate `np II`, `P_18` = `1`.
- Services to non-EU businesses → rate `np I`, `P_18` = `1`.
- WDT (goods to the EU) → rate `0 WDT`, `P_18` = `2`.
- Export of goods → rate `0 EX`, `P_18` = `2`.
- **Never** use the `oo` rate for foreign counterparties.
- Verify the buyer's EU VAT number in [VIES](https://ec.europa.eu/taxation_customs/vies/) before issuing the invoice.
