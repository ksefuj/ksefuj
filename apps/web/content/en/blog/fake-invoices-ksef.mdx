You log in to KSeF (Krajowy System e-Faktur — Poland's National e-Invoicing System) and find an invoice from a company you've never heard of. For a service you never ordered. Amount: 3,500 PLN. Payment due: 7 days.

Before you do anything — take a breath. **An invoice appearing in KSeF creates no tax obligation for you on its own.** Tax consequences arise only when you book the document in your accounts. If you haven't booked it, you're in the clear — at least as far as the tax authority is concerned.

This is a real problem, but it's not a disaster. Here's how to handle it — calmly, step by step.

---

## Why this happens

KSeF assigns invoices to the recipient automatically — based on the NIP (Polish tax identification number) that the issuer enters on the document. There is no "accept" mechanism on your side: the invoice appears in your account before you have any chance to react.

Your NIP is publicly available — in the CEIDG register (for sole traders — JDG) and in the KRS (for companies). It takes seconds to look up. This isn't your fault and it isn't a flaw in KSeF as such — it's simply a gap that some dishonest actors have been trying to exploit since mandatory e-invoicing began.

For more on how [KSeF works technically and legally, see our full compendium](/blog/ksef-od-1-kwietnia-2026).

## Honest mistake or scam — how to tell

An unknown invoice in KSeF isn't always fraudulent. Sometimes someone simply entered the wrong NIP — transposed two digits, confused similar numbers. Before you report it as abuse, work out which situation you're actually dealing with.

### Scenario A: wrong NIP

The issuer entered your NIP instead of the correct recipient's NIP. This is a mistake, not fraud. In this case:

- contact the seller and let them know about the error,
- ask them to issue a zero-value corrective invoice cancelling the original document,
- the seller should then issue a new invoice with the correct NIP.

For the formal details of what that correction looks like — see the article [Corrective Invoices in KSeF — How to Fix a Mistake](/blog/faktura-korygujaca-ksef).

### Scenario B: deliberate abuse

If none of the details add up — the company, the service, anything — you're likely dealing with an attempted fraud. Watch out for these warning signs:

- The company on the invoice is completely unknown to you and you've had no prior contact.
- The described service or goods mean nothing to you — you didn't order them and don't even know what they are.
- When you try calling or emailing the seller, the phone rings out and the email bounces.
- The payment deadline is absurdly short — three days, five days — as if someone is banking on panic and haste.
- The service description contains odd phrasing or unnatural language, as though it was machine-translated.
- The seller doesn't appear on the Biała Lista (Poland's VAT taxpayer register — [Wykaz Podatników VAT](https://www.podatki.gov.pl/wykaz-podatnikow-vat-wyszukiwarka)), or their status shows as "inactive".

<Warning>
  None of these signals — individually or together — constitute an official MF checklist. They are
  practical indicators to help you assess the situation. If in doubt, consult an accountant or tax
  adviser.
</Warning>

## What to do — step by step

### Step 1: Don't pay, and don't book it

This is the single most important thing to understand: **the mere presence of an invoice in KSeF does not obligate you to pay or to deduct VAT.** Under Art. 86 ust. 10 of the Polish VAT Act (right to deduct input VAT), the right to deduct arises in the settlement period when the invoice is entered into the accounts — not when it appears in KSeF. Additionally, Art. 88 ust. 3a pkt 4 of the Polish VAT Act (exclusions from VAT deduction) rules out any right to deduct VAT from invoices that document transactions which never actually took place.

Haven't booked it? The invoice sits in your KSeF inbox and does precisely nothing.

<Info>
  If you use an accounting firm or invoicing software with automatic import from KSeF — act
  immediately. Don't wait until the end of the month. A suspicious invoice can be booked before
  you've had a chance to flag it.
</Info>

If your software imports documents from KSeF automatically — check whether that invoice has already made it into your system. If it has: let your accountant know so they can remove it from the records before the period closes.

### Step 2: Check the seller on the Biała Lista

Before anything else, go to the [Wykaz Podatników VAT](https://www.podatki.gov.pl/wykaz-podatnikow-vat-wyszukiwarka) and search for the seller's NIP from the invoice.

Active entry, details consistent with the invoice, address and account numbers all match — it's possible this is just a genuine mistake. Worth trying to get in touch.

No entry, inactive status, details don't match — that's a strong signal you're dealing with an attempted fraud. Don't contact the seller. Move to step 3.

### Step 3: Report the abuse in the Aplikacja Podatnika

From 1 April 2026, the Aplikacja Podatnika KSeF (the built-in taxpayer portal at [ap.ksef.mf.gov.pl](https://ap.ksef.mf.gov.pl/)) includes a simplified mechanism for reporting suspicious invoices. You don't need to write letters to the tax office — a few clicks in the application is all it takes.

How to do it:

1. Log in to the Aplikacja Podatnika KSeF at [ap.ksef.mf.gov.pl](https://ap.ksef.mf.gov.pl/).
2. Locate the suspicious invoice in your inbox.
3. Use the "report abuse" option available on the document.
4. Provide a brief explanation — just describe why you consider the invoice illegitimate (e.g. "I don't know this company and I didn't order this service").

A few things worth knowing about this mechanism, based on the KSeF 2.0 Manual and MF FAQ:

**One report = one invoice.** You can't flag several documents at once in a single form. If you received three suspicious invoices, you need to submit three separate reports.

**Only the recipient can report.** The function is available exclusively to the buyer named on the invoice. The seller cannot report their own invoice this way.

**KAS (National Revenue Administration) reviews reports, but per the MF FAQ it does not inform you of the outcome.** Submitting a report does not mean you'll receive a confirmation or any update on whether action was taken.

**The report is technical and administrative in nature.** As far as available MF information indicates, this mechanism is not separately regulated in the VAT Act as a distinct legal procedure — it is a technical tool within the Aplikacja Podatnika.

**You can withdraw a report.** If after filing you discover it was actually a mistake — the seller got in touch, explained the situation, and issued a correction — you have the option to withdraw the report.

<Tip>
  Reporting via the Aplikacja Podatnika is an administrative tool — it does not replace a formal
  complaint to law enforcement if you suspect a criminal offence. In that case, consult a lawyer or
  tax adviser.
</Tip>

### Step 4: Let your accountant know

Even if you use an accounting firm that "handles KSeF for you" — it's still your job to flag a suspicious document. Why does this matter?

Your accountant can see your invoices, but they don't know your contracts. They have no way of knowing you didn't order this. If they have automatic KSeF import enabled, they may book the invoice in the course of routine work — especially at month-end, when they're processing dozens of documents at once.

Give them a clear message: **"This invoice is suspicious. Please do not book it until this is resolved."**

While you're at it, ask whether the software your firm uses has a filter that flags invoices from unknown counterparties before they are automatically imported into the accounts.

## How to protect yourself going forward

There's no foolproof way to stop someone entering your NIP on a fraudulent invoice. But a few simple habits significantly reduce the chance of something going wrong:

**Check KSeF regularly.** Don't wait until the end of the month. Glance at your inbox every few days — especially if your accounting firm imports invoices periodically. The sooner you spot a suspicious document, the less likely it is to be accidentally booked.

**Agree a procedure with your accountant.** Before the first automatic KSeF import, agree on how to handle invoices from unknown counterparties. A simple rule — "any new company needs confirmation from me first" — is enough.

**Don't panic at every unfamiliar name.** Mistakes happen. Not every unknown invoice is a crime — sometimes it's a typo in a NIP, or a seller's error that gets sorted out quickly.

If you issue your own invoices — check them before sending to KSeF. An invoice with the wrong buyer NIP can land on the account of someone who has never heard of you — and you'll be the seller in exactly this kind of story. ## Watch out for phishing

Since [mandatory KSeF went live](/blog/ksef-ruszyl), a wave of emails impersonating the Ministry of Finance or KSeF has been circulating. The messages look official, contain links to "log in", and ask for your credentials.

The rule is simple: **never click links in emails about KSeF.** Always go directly to [ksef.podatki.gov.pl](https://ksef.podatki.gov.pl/) — by typing the address manually or using a saved bookmark. Official system notifications don't require you to click confirmation links.

## Summary

Fake invoices in KSeF are a real problem — but they're not a disaster. An invoice sitting in your account creates no obligation to pay and no tax liability, as long as you don't book it. Check the seller on the Biała Lista, don't pay for anything you didn't order, and report the suspicious document in the Aplikacja Podatnika. If you use an accounting firm — call them before they open their next batch of documents to import. Checking KSeF regularly and having a simple procedure in place with your accountant is the best protection you can give yourself.
